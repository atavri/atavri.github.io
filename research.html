Research

My research focuses on understanding how physical processes in transitional sea ice regimes—such as melt, freeze-up, and wave–ice interaction—reshape surface structure and influence satellite observations. I combine multi-sensor satellite data, field campaigns, and coupled models to link physical processes with their observable signatures across scales.

A central theme of my work is that satellite retrieval biases are not simply limitations of observation, but reflect real, regime-dependent changes in surface physics. By connecting thermodynamic evolution, wave–ice interactions, and upper-ocean processes to satellite signal behavior, I aim to transform these biases into physically interpretable diagnostics of a changing polar system.

Melt-Season Microwave Signatures

During the melt season, sea ice undergoes rapid thermodynamic transitions—snow metamorphism, brine evolution, and melt pond formation—that fundamentally alter surface roughness and dielectric properties. These changes reshape how microwave signals interact with sea ice, reducing the validity of winter-based retrieval assumptions.

  • Quantifying frequency-dependent scattering using C- and L-band SAR.
  • Linking melt processes to transitions between surface and volume scattering.
  • Tracking melt evolution using multi-sensor observations (Sentinel-1, SWOT).

Wave–Ice Interactions in the MIZ

In the marginal ice zone, ocean waves fragment sea ice and increase sub-footprint heterogeneity. These dynamic processes modify surface roughness, floe size distribution, and upper-ocean mixing, directly impacting satellite observables and retrieval consistency.

  • Relating wave-driven fragmentation to retrieval discrepancies near the ice edge.
  • Using SAR to quantify surface roughness and floe-scale variability.
  • Coupling observations with neXtSIM–WaveWatch III simulations.

Diagnosing Retrieval Biases in Observation Space

Passive microwave sea ice concentration is inferred from brightness temperatures rather than directly observed. I develop physics-constrained forward models to evaluate retrieval biases in observation space, allowing inconsistencies to be traced back to underlying surface conditions.

  • Forward-emulating brightness temperatures from physical surface states.
  • Identifying regime-dependent biases during melt and in dynamic ice conditions.
  • Integrating SAR, ICESat-2, and wave models to constrain uncertainty.

Linking Processes, Observations, and Models

Across these efforts, my work builds a unified framework that connects thermodynamic evolution, dynamic forcing, and upper-ocean processes to satellite signal behavior. This approach enables retrieval biases to be understood as predictable outcomes of changing surface conditions.

  • Bridging scales from field observations to basin-scale satellite records.
  • Improving interpretation of remote sensing products in climate studies.
  • Supporting physically consistent model–data integration.
