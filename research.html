Current Research

Combining satellite remote sensing, process-level physics, and coupled models to understand sea-ice variability and improve climate representations.

Themes

From Sea-Ice Floes to the Ice Pack

Variability from floe to basin scale with emphasis on the marginal ice zone (MIZ) where breakup and waves drive lateral melt and mixing. I connect floe-scale physics—roughness, melt ponds, snow—to basin-scale constraints on heat, momentum, and freshwater exchange.

Microwave Scattering Across Melt

Using polarimetric SAR (C and L band) to separate surface and volume scattering through the melt season, extending the usable window of remote-sensing climate records. Field validation (e.g., MOSAiC) ties SAR signatures to albedo and energy-flux changes.

Biases in Passive-Microwave SIC

Forward-emulating brightness temperatures (Tb) from model SIC and comparing with AMSR2/SSMIS reveals regime-dependent algorithmic biases. Coupling SAR, ICESat-2, and wave hindcasts helps diagnose and correct wave-driven errors in SIC retrievals.

Turbulence, Waves & the OSBL

neXtSIM–WaveWatch III experiments quantify how wind–wave misalignment and partial ice cover shape vertical kinetic energy, mixing, and Langmuir turbulence beneath sea ice—informing physically consistent mixing parameterizations in climate models.
